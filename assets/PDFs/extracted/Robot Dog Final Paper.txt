Introduction 
A quadruped robot dog, Squeaky, was developed to address a signiﬁcant gap in K-12 
education: the lack of aƯordable, durable and approachable robotic platforms. 
Commercially available robot dogs often cost between $600 and $2000, making them far 
beyond the budget of most schools, after-school programs, and families. As Program-
Director at Coco Academy, a makerspace for kids based in South Philadelphia, the need for 
low-cost hands-on robotic tools became clear.  Squeaky was created to meet this need, 
providing both ﬁnancial accessibility and entry level capabilities for students of all ages. To 
achieve this goal, the project was designed around four key requirements: maintaining a 
total cost under $100, ensuring durability under rough handling, supporting easy assembly 
and repair, and incorporating an intuitive custom block-based interface 
Mechanical Design 
The mechanical design of the robot dog focused on strength and ease of repair. The robot 
used an eight-degree-of-freedom conﬁguration with two MG996R high-torque servos 
attached to each leg to control the hip and knee joints. 
 
Figure 1: A CAD Model of the Robot Dog 
 An embedded servo leg architecture was chosen over a linkage-based leg design because 
it reduces assembly complexity and simpliﬁes the inverse kinematics in the movement 
logic. All structural parts were designed in Onshape, a browser-based CAD application, and 
3D-printed in PLA due to its low cost and adequate stiƯness. Layer lines were intentionally 
oriented to maximize strength and aluminum alloy servo attachments were added to the 
joints to ensure a strong connection. To support ease of assembly and repair, the robot 
uses only two screw types and features modular components that can be easily replaced 
or customized.  
Electrical Design  
The electrical system was created to focus on real-time responsiveness, low cost and 
simple integration. The Arduino Uno R4 Wi-Fi microcontroller was selected as the “brain” of 
the robot due to its built-in ESP32-S3 wireless module and its aƯordability compared to 
Raspberry Pi alternatives.  
  
Figure 2: A picture of the Arduino and breakboard used in the robot 
The microcontroller is attached to a V5 sensor shield which is used as a breakout board to 
easily attach all of the servos without complex wiring. Servos were chosen over DC or 
stepper motors because they include internal potentiometer for position feedback and 
consume less power. The electrical system is powered by a tethered power supply either to 
a PC or a wall outlet which allows for easy debugging and no time requirement for charging. 
The conﬁguration results in a simple but robust system that provides a wireless connection 
while keeping cost to a minimum. 
  
Durability Testing 
Durability was a central requirement throughout the design process as the robot must be 
able to withstand impacts, drops and rough handling to allow for hours of endless learning. 
Since most of the frame is 3D-printed, the structural integrity needs to be validated. Finite 
Element Analysis (FEA) studies were conducted in SimScale on the upper and lower 
portions of the leg as they are the most likely to be pulled or struck during use. In each 
study, two trials were made by changing the force from 50N (~11lbs) to 100N (~22lbs). From 
each trial, the highest Von Mises stress is found and using the Yield Strength of PLA, 40 
MPa, the factor of safety is found. The ﬁrst study conducted focused on the upper leg 
simulating a student squeezing the legs inwards. 
 
Figure 3: Upper Leg, Distributed Load, FEA Model and Results Plot 
A ﬁxed support was made at the servo connection, and the force was applied towards the 
center of the dog on the outer face. The factor of safety from each trial was 11.9 and 5.96, 
respectively. The next study also focused on the upper leg but had a point force directed at 
the bottom of the part simulating bumping into a wall or falling oƯ a table.  
  
Figure 4: Upper Leg. Point load, FEA Model and Results Plot 
A ﬁxed support was made at the servo connection, and the forces were applied as a point 
load towards the servo connection at the bottom of the part. The factor of safety from each 
trial was 29.7 and 14.9, respectively. The third study focused on the bottom lab with a 
distributed load along the inner face simulating a study picking up the dog by the leg.  
    
Figure 5: Lower Leg, Distributed Load, FEA Model and Results Plot 
A ﬁxed support was made at the servo connection, and the forces were applied outwards 
to the inner face of the leg. The factor of safety for each trial was 3.56 and 1.78, 
respectively. Since the factor of safety is lower than 2, there could be some structural 
integrity issues that arise; however, since the force applied, 100N, exceeds that of normal 
operational use, it is safe to assume that the leg will not break under normal use. The last 
study focused on the lower leg but with a point contact at the bottom of the leg to simulate 
the robot being dropped or falling oƯ a table.  
 
Figure 6: Lower Leg, Point Contact, FEA Model and Results Plot 
A ﬁxed support was placed at the servo connection, and a point contact was applied 
towards the connection from the bottom of the leg. The factor of safety for each trial was 
29.7 and 14.9, respectively. Across all simulations, the minimum factor of safety under 
extreme conditions remained 1.78, conﬁrming that the 3D-printed structure provides 
suƯicient durability for everyday classroom use. 
  
Control Logic 
The control logic uses a set of inverse kinematic equations to control the precise 
movement of the servos. First, the robot dog must undergo a calibration procedure in 
which each servo is set to 90 degrees. Each servo must be adjusted until each limb is 
perpendicular to each other. The adjusted neutral position is saved as a calibration oƯset 
and later applied to all computer angles to ensure consistency in each step. The leg 
mechanism is modeled as a two-link planar system and uses the known leg dimensions in 
the inverse kinematics equations.     
 
Figure 7: The Inverse Kinematic Calculations for the Leg model. 
The inverse kinematic equations are implemented in C++ to compute the target angles for 
each joint based on a desired foot position in the X-Y space.  
 
Figure 8: Implemented Inverse Kinematics 
Once computed, the Arduino applies the calibration oƯset and sends a ﬁnal angle 
command to the servos. This approach enables smooth leg motion while keeping 
computation demand low enough for the microcontroller.  
  
User Interface 
To make the robot dog accessible for younger students, a custom drag-and-drop interface 
was developed using a lightweight web-based architecture. The ESP32-S3 module on the 
Arduino hosts a small HTTP server that serves an HTML-based control dashboard stored 
directly in ﬂash memory. The user connects to the robot over Wi-Fi and can interact with a 
scratch-style coding interface that allows them to control motion without text-based code. 
The interface includes movement commands such as forward, backward, turning, sitting, 
standing, as well as logical operators like loops and wait functions.  
Figure 9: Custom Block-Based Interface 
 This design prioritizes conceptual understanding of coding and algorithmic thinking which 
reduces frustration associated with syntax errors. Students can rapidly prototype 
sequences of movements and see immediate results, engaging and gamifying the learning 
experience.

Costs can be further reduced through sourcing parts from wholesale suppliers such as 
AliExpress or Alibaba which makes the sub-$100 target practical for bulk production. To 
consider other potential costs during production such as manufacturing time, assembly 
and packaging, a 2.5x markup can be applied to achieve a market comparison. The value of 
a 2.5x markup was found to be a industry guideline for hardware products. Under this 
model, a reasonable retail price would be approximately $260 which is still far below 
competing quadruped kits on the market such as Petoi Bittle ($329) and Mangdang 
Minipupper ($604). This cost structure positions this robot dog as one of the most 
aƯordable robotic platforms available for K-12 education.  
Conclusion 
The quadruped robot dog successfully meets all the key requirements established at the 
start of the project: aƯordability, durability, ease of assembly and repair, and compatibility 
with a drag-and-drop coding system. By keeping the total material cost near the $100 
target, the design addresses the need for ﬁnancially accessible robotic platforms in K-12 
education. Durability through FEA demonstrated that the 3D-printed components will hold 
up under rough handling from younger students. The modular mechanical structure, 
simple screw selection, and easily replaceable parts ensure that users can maintain the 
robot with minimal downtime. Lastly, the implementation of a wireless, self-contained, 
block-based programming interface allows control of the robot without the frustration of 
traditional syntax-based coding. With future improvements such as enhanced walking 
algorithms, additional sensors, and expanded coding features, the robot dog has strong 
potential to grow into a widely adopted educational tool that helps young students develop 
engineering intuition and computational thinking skills.
